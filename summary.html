Hello! Thank you for visiting the SPT-3G data quality summary webpage and for reading the documentation here. If you notice that the figures in either the Calibration Observations tab or the Field Observations tab have not been updated for the last three days (but have not heard of any issues with the operation of the telescope and the camera), or if you have some questions or suggestions on the webpage, please let Adam and Wei know!


1. Overview

This webpage is meant to be used to monitor how stable the results of various observations have been over time, especially for the last several days. Each figure (except for some figures in the Field Observations tab) shows the time variations of certain quantity during certain time interval. If the operation of the telescope and the camera was normal during that time interval, then the data points plotted there should show a flat trend; the presence of any abnormal data point indicates that there was some issue with the operation (or perhaps some bug in our analysis pipeline that calculated that data point).

There are not any strict definitions on what is considered as abnormal, but, if you notice that almost all the results from last few days' observations deviate from the general trends over the past month by more than 50%, then that is propably something worth investigating. There are some additional suggestions in this wiki page as to what is considered as abnormal. Also, if you want to know the current status of the northern shifts for data quality monitoring, please check this wiki page.

With this tool, we hope that any potentially serious problem will be detected early on! The following sections have some explanations on the figures shown on this webpage.


2. Calibration Observations tab

This tab contains figures showing the time variations of various calibration quantities' medians from different observing bands of different wafers during different time intervals. These calibration quantities are simply the ones that are computed by the auto-processing pipeline. By default, the medians from all wafers are shown. The presence of a red triangle near either the top or bottom of a plot indicates that there is a data point that lies outside the range, and the presence of a vertical red line indicates that there is a NaN.

If you are not familiar with what types of calibration observations are taken, you may find the following resources useful: the documentation on calibration in the spt3g_software repository, Nathan's slides, Daniel's wiki page, and Tom's wiki page.


2.1. Calibrator section

The figures in the left column show the quantity CalibratorResponse computed by the auto-processing, those in the center column show CalibratorResponseSN, and those in the right colunm show numbers of bolometers whose CalibratorResponseSN were above 20, which is the threshold used in our map-making pipeline to remove bolometers that had low response to the calibrator.

Results from calibrator stares are separated according to the telescope elevation at which each stare took place. The figures in the first (second) row show the results from those stares that were taken within the range of the elevation covered in a "low-el" observing "day" ("high-el" observing "day"), in which we observe the ra0hdec-44.75 and ra0hdec-52.25 sub-fields (ra0hdec-59.75 and ra0hdec-67.25 sub-fields). Here, "low-el" and "high-el" refer to the ranges [40, 56] and [56, 72] degrees, respectively, and a "day" lasts for about 20 hours (fridge cycle time plus fridge hold time).

(One minor complication to what is stated in the previous paragraph is that the figures in the first row actually show some results from high-el observing days as well, and, likewise, the figures from the second row show some results from low-el observing days. This is because we take a calibrator stare at the bottom elevation of a sub-field right before observing that field and then another calibrator stare at the top elevation right after finishing observing that field. Since the top elevation of the ra0hdec-52.25 sub-field is slightly above 56 degrees, and the bottom elevation of the ra0hdec-59.75 sub-field is slightly below 56 degrees, the figures in both rows end up showing results from both types of observing days.)

In addition to the separation by elevation, the results from those special calibrator stares that were taken at unusual elevations (i.e. any elevation higher than 72 or lower than 40 degrees) or with unsual chopper frequencies (i.e. any frequency other than 4 Hz) are excluded. This is to avoid potential confusions caused by showing results from standard stares and non-standard ones together. The end result of this filtering is that each data point shown here corresponds to a standard calibrator stare that was taken as part of a normal schedule to observe a sub-field.


2.2. Elnod section

The left figure shows the quantity ElnodSNSlopes from the auto-processing, the middle one shows the IQ phase angle, which is given by arctan(ElnodEigenvalueDominantVectorQ / ElnodEigenvalueDominantVectorI), and the right one shows numbers of bolometers whose ElnodSNSlopes were larger than 20. The periodic variations of ElnodSNSlopes seem to be closely related to the elevations at which the elnods were taken; the values are larger on low-el observing days and smaller on high-el observing days. The reason ElnodSNSlopes are larger for elnods taken at lower elevations is perhaps because there is more change in airmass during an elnod taken at a lower elevation than at a higher one.


2.3. HII Regions section

The figures in this section show the quantities SourceFluxCalibration, SourceIntegralFlux, and SourceSkyTransmission, where Source can be either RCW38 or MAT5A. The first two quantites are computed from fast-point observations, and the last one from very-fast-point observations. RCW38 is used to calibrate observations of the two low-el fields, and MAT5A for the two high-el fields.

Fast-point observations provide the main ingredient we need to calibrate bolometer timestreams into K_CMB units. Given that these observations are taken only once in about a week, it is very important to make sure that the results from each fast-point do not look bad. The medians of IntegralFlux and FluxCalibration should not look abnormal. In addition, the numbers of detectors whose FluxCalibration have negative values should not be much smaller than usual (since FluxCalibration is defined as a bolometer's peak response to the source (the most negative value in its map of the source expressed in some units of electrical bias power) divided by its reaponse to the calibrator (a positive quantity), this number at least needs to be negative for it to be considered as reasonable).


2.4. Noise section

This section shows bolometers' noise levels in three different units (noise equivalent temperature (NET), noise equivalent power (NEP), and noise equivalent current (NEI)) within four different frequency ranges ([0.1, 0.5], [1.0, 2.0], [3.0, 5.0], and [10.0, 15.0] Hz). These numbers come from the auto-processing of the daily noise stares' timestreams. The noise levels in the lowest frequency range are more susceptible to weather than those in the other three ranges, and thus they are expected to show more variations.


2.5. Figures for individual observations

In every figure shown in the Calibration Observations tab, one observation's results are reduced to one data point (for each observing band). If you are interested in checking the details of the results from certain observation, say, the distribution of CalibratorResponse from a particular calibrator stare, you may find the per-observation plotter useful. Instructions on how to use this webpage can be found in Adam's slides.


3. Field Observations tab

In this tab, for each time interval listed on the left, you can find figures showing co-added maps from that interval and figures showing time variations of several map-related quantities such as map noise level during the same interval. In the latter type of figure, one data point corresponds to one field observation. The yearly intervals actually have different types of figures, which are explained in the following sections.

Most of the figures have the notations "el0", "el1", "el2", and "el3" in their legends and/or titles, and these are simply shorthands for the ra0hdec-44.75, ra0hdec-52.25, ra0hdec-59.75, and ra0hdec-67.25 sub-fields, respectively. The figure below shows the extent of the 1500 square degrees field in RA and Dec. The three constant-Dec contours separate the full field into four sub-fields, the top of which is the "el0", and the bottom of which is the "el3". The shaded regions are the ones used for auto and cross spectrum calculations, which are explained more later.


3.1. Maps section

The figures in the first row show co-added temperature maps from each of the three observing bands. The lower and upper limits of the color bars are determined in different ways for different types of time intervals.

For the Recent and Weekly time intervals, a map, which has 0.25 arcminute resolution, is first smoothed with a 1.0 arcminute FWHM Gaussian, and then the 1st and 99th percentile values in the smoothed map are used as the lower and upper limits of the color bar, respectively. The smoothing is done because, if the original map, which has 18000 by 12000 pixels, is shown in an image that has about 1500 by 1000 pixels, the map in the resultant image seems to look too noisy to serve useful purposes such as revealing some strange features if they exist.

For the Yearly and Monthly time intervals, the map does not look noisy enough in the resultant image to require smoothing, so that processing is not done. As for the color bar limits, they are determined not directly from the actual values in a map but from expectations. The expectations are based on a model that the variance of the values in a map is equal to the variance from the noise plus the variance from the signal. While the former should be inversely proportional to the number of maps added together, the latter should be a constant. From a typical noise variance, an educated guess for the signal variance, the number of maps added together, and this model, an expected variance is calculated for a co-add, and the color bar limits are set to be +/- 2.5 times the corresponding standard deviation.

The image below shows an example of how the expected variance is determined. The blue data points show how the variance of a 2019 co-added signal (plus noise) map changed everytime two new maps were added, and the orange data points show how the variance of the corresponding co-added noise map changed. The blue box, whisker, and caps in the upper left corner show a distribution of variances of individual maps divided by two. The blue and orange curves are the results of curve fittings to the data points, and the models used in the fittings are shown in the legend.



The median of the blue distribution is used as the "typical noise variance" mentioned in the previous paragraph, and the asymptote of the blue curve is used as the "educated guess for the signal variance". These fittings are done for each observing band and each sub-field, and all of these results are taken into account when an expected variance of a full-field co-add is calculated.

The figures in the second row show co-added TT weight maps. The lower and upper limits of a color bar in this type of figures simply correspond to the minimum and maximum of the values in a weight map, respectively. The figures in the third row show cross sections of these weight maps, and the values are normalized to the weight at Dec = -70 degrees. The cross section is along the RA = 0 degree contour, which corresponds to the vertical line that bisects a map. Along with the actual data, expected cross sections are also shown, which are based on a simple model that the weight of a pixel is proportional to the amount of time the telescope spends on staring at the sky area that corresponds to the pixel. Since our scans have the same duration and cover the same RA range regardless of declination (or the telescope elevation), the time spent on a pixel is inversely proportional to cosine of the declination of that pixel. This time spent is also proportional to the number of observations that covered that pixel. As a result, the expected cross section shown in a figure is basically equal to secant of declination multiplied by number of observation.


3.2. Fluctuations in Maps section

For non-yearly time intervals, there are two rows of figures in this section. The first row shows noise levels, and the second row shows averages of TT weights. The noise level of a temperature map is defined here as the average of square root of C_l of the autospectrum in the ell range [3000, 5000]. In order to save time and reduce memory usage, only the data within a 5 degrees by 5 degrees patch located around the center of a sub-field are used for calculating the noise. These small patches do contain bright point sources, which are masked during the calculations. As for calculating an average of a TT weight map, the peripheral regions of the map where weights sharply decrease toward zero are ignored. The figures below show the mask used for each of the sub-field and the effect of masking the sources on the autospectrum calculation (the maps shown here are not from co-adds but just from some random single-observation maps). The reason the masking makes a much bigger difference for the ra0hdec-52.25 sub-field seems to be because the point sources in the field are much brighter.

For yearly time intervals, there are three rows of figures in this section. The second row shows histograms of the noise levels of individual temperature maps, which is a complementary way of visualizing the data shown in the non-yearly time intervals. The third row shows histograms of the averages of individual weight maps. In these figures, the mode, median (MED), and median absolute deviation (MAD) of each histogram are printed.

The first row is somewhat unique; it shows the time evolution of the noise levels of the running year-to-date co-added temperature maps. The data points shown there are calculated as follows: existing maps are separated into two groups to form two co-adds, and newly made maps are alternately added to one of these two co-adds; then, whenever both co-adds have the same number of maps, their weights are removed, the difference between the two is taken, and noise is calculated from the difference map after it is divided by two. This procedure is somewhat similar to making a co-add by alternately adding and subtracting maps, which is how the x-axis label got its name. The noise levels in the running year-to-date co-adds are expected to be inversely proportional to square root of the number of maps used, which is why log-log plots are used here, and also results from linear least squares fitting to the logarithm of the data are shown.


3.3. Pointing section

For non-yearly time intervals, time variations of discrepancies between measured positions and true possitions of three bright point sources from each sub-field are shown. The measured positions are results of 2D Gaussian fittings to small map regions around the sources, and the true positions are from the ATCA20G catalog. The image below shows the locations of these point sources.

The signal-to-noise ratios of these sources are smaller in 220 GHz maps than in 95 and 150 GHz, but the numbers are nearly the same in 95 GHz and 150 GHz, and figures for the latter are chosen to be shown. Of these twelve sources, the brightest one is in the ra0hdec-52.25 sub-field and has SNR of a few hundreds. The dimmest one is in the ra0hdec-67.25 sub-field and has SNR of about 15. The other ten sources' SNRs are roughly in the range between 25 and 70 (SNR here is defined as the peak value of a source divided by a standard deviation of the sub-field map to which the source belongs, and the standard deviation is calculated by masking point sources).

There are four figures for RA offsets (one for each sub-field) and four for Dec offsets. In each figure, along with the offsets from the three sources, the average offset of these three sources is also shown. For yearly time intervals, there are figures showing distributions of the average offsets.


3.4. Completeness of Observations section

This section exists for non-yearly time intervals only. The first row shows average numbers of bolometers that were not flagged and thus used for map-making, bolometers that were flagged and removed from map-making, and bolometers that were flagged for different reasons. The map-making pipeline does bolometer flagging on a scan-by-scan basis, so here an average number refers to the average over all scans of an observation. Since one bolometer can be flagged for different reasons, the sum of the average numbers of bolometers flagged by individual reasons exceed the average number of flagged and removed bolometers (the red crosses) for each observation. As for the numbers of removed bolometers, most of them are just those bolometers that we do not attempt to tune to begin with. This can be seen from the more or less constant levels of "BadHK". Thus, the figures do not indicate that there are typically about one thousand bolometers from each band that were initially tuned successfully but later produced bad data during observing.

The figures in the second row show fractions of pixels in TT weight maps that have nominal weights. Currently, there are suggestions that the criteria used to determine whether a weight value is nominal or not are too strict and should be loosened, and these figures will be regenerated soon, so please ignore them for now.

The figure in the third row shows the duration of each observation as recorded in its g3 files. This figure also happens to show our observing cadence in a colorful way. The printed observing efficiency is calculated by dividing the total observation duration from that time interval by the length of the entire interval.


3.5. Temperature Calibration section

For a non-yearly time interval, there are four rows of figures in this section. The first row compares the temperature calibration of each of our maps against that of Planck's maps. Specifically, for each temperature map, the average ratio of two spectra in the ell range [750, 1250] is calculated; the numerator is the cross spectrum between a T map of ours and the Planck's full mission map, and the denominator is the cross spectrum between one of Planck's half mission map and the other half mission map. If our temperature calibration is reasonably close to that of Planck's, this ratio should be close to 1.0. Since this calculation is not sophisticated enough to apply corrections due to our beams, etc., how close the numbers are to 1.0 is perhaps not a very meaningful aspect to pay attention to. However, the figures should still be useful to catch those maps that have abnormal calibration. Like the noise calculations, in order to save time and memory usage, only the small regions around the cetners of the sub-field are used for these cross spectrum calculations (points sources are masked as well). The figure below shows an example of calculating this ratio. The SPT map used here is not a co-add but just a random single-observation map.

The second and third rows show by how much bolometers' response to the calibrator vary as the telescope elevation changes. As part of the schedule to observe a sub-field, we take a calibrator stare at the bottom elevation of that field before observing it and then take another calibrator stare at the top elevation of that field after observing it. From the auto-processed results of these two calibrator stares, the percentage change in the quantity CalibratorResponse at the top of the field with respect to the bottom is calculated for each bolometer. Then, the figures in the second row show the median percentage change from all bolometers of each observing band, and the figures in the third row show the median from each wafer and each observing band.

The figures in the fourth row show the conversion factors we use to calibrate bolometers' timestreams from some units of power into K_CMB. The median value of pW/K from each wafer is shown. A pW/K conversion factor is equal to the product of CalibratorResponse, FluxCalibration, IntegralFlux, and SkyTransmission divided by some constant. If a figure here shows that certain observation had abnormal pW/K conversion factors, you may be able to find out which of the four factors led to the abonrmality by checking the figures in the Calibration Observations tab.

For a yearly time interval, there are two rows of figures in this section. The first row shows histograms of the ratios of the cross spectra, and the second row shows the median percentage changes in bolometers' response to the calibrator.


4. Repository

All the relevant scripts that work together to make this webpage run are located in the spt_dq_system repository. db_server.js is the Java script that starts a server that serves this webpage. It calls the python script update_summary.py periodically to update the figures shown in this webpage. When update_summary.py is invoked, it in turn calls the scripts in the spt_dq_system/summaryplot directory that do the actual work of updating relevant data products and figures.

This updating actually happens only at Pole; the data products, such as pickle files storing the medians of the calibration quantities and g3 files storing co-added maps and analysis results, are updated automatically and periodically only at Pole. As for the calibration observations, the pickle files are transferred to Amundsen/Scott and stored in /spt/data/rsync/spt_dq_data/. Then, the figures are made from these pickles in the north. As for the field observations, though, the g3 files are not transferred due to their sizes; it is only the figures that are transferred, which are located in /spt/data/rsync/spt_dq_maps_figures/. However, there is a test version of the webpage running on Amundsen. Even though the updating is only done occasionally for this version, the g3 files are made on Amundsen and thus readily accessible to anyone in the north who is interested in using them. They are located in /spt/user/weiquan/map_quality/hi_res_maps/.
